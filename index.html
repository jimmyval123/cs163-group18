// OVERVIEW

Project Summary

Motivation

League of Legends is one of the world's most-played games, yet winning remains poorly understood at a statistical level. Conventional wisdom says "get kills to win" — our analysis of 635K+ Challenger matches shows this is far too simplistic. Success is champion-specific, not universally defined.

Approach

We built individual logistic regression models for each of 172 champions using 16 standardized performance features. Coefficient vectors were then clustered via K-Means (k=6) and visualized with PCA to reveal structural groupings across the champion pool.

Key Finding: Champions like Jinx (Marksman) win through KDA and objective damage, while supports like Braum win through crowd control and mitigation — not kills. Our per-champion models capture these role-specific win conditions with high accuracy (AUC ≈ 0.92 on average).

// RESEARCH QUESTIONS

Research Questions

Question 01

Performance Stats & Win Probability

How do individual performance statistics (KDA, damage, vision, objectives) correlate with a champion's probability of winning in Challenger-tier play? We hypothesize that the most predictive features are role-dependent.

Question 02

Clustering by Win Profile

Can champions be meaningfully grouped based on similarities in the features that predict their victories, independent of official role assignments? We use K-Means on coefficient vectors to find natural groupings.

Question 03

Clusters vs. Official Archetypes

Do data-driven clusters correspond to Riot's official classifications? Partial alignment would validate our approach; divergence reveals novel insights about non-standard champion playstyles.

// EXPLORATORY DATA ANALYSIS

Data Overview

Feature Engineering

Four derived features were engineered: nonCsGold (gold excluding minion farm), damageEfficiency (damage per gold), mitigationRatio (damage blocked vs. taken), and physicalDamageRatio (physical vs. total damage). These capture playstyle dimensions raw stats miss.

Feature Selection

Reduced from 27 candidate features to 16 by removing redundant signals: kills/deaths/assists encoded via kda and assistRatio; goldEarned captured via nonCsGold; raw minion counts mainly encode role, not within-role variation; totalDamageTaken captured relatively by mitigationRatio.

// ANALYTICAL METHODS

Modeling Approach

Per-Champion Logistic Regression

A separate logistic regression (L2 penalty, C=1.0) was trained for each of 172 champions with ≥50 games. Features were z-score standardized before fitting, making coefficients comparable across features within a champion. The binary target is win (1) vs. loss (0), evaluated with AUC-ROC.

Clustering & PCA

Coefficient vectors (172 × 16) were assembled into a matrix and clustered with K-Means (k=6). PCA was applied for 2D visualization. PC1 explains 27.8% of variance, PC2 explains 22.5% — 50.3% total, capturing the dominant structure in champion win profiles.

PC1 (27.8%) roughly separates damage-dealers from utility champions; PC2 (22.5%) captures within-role variation.

Finding 1 — Win Conditions Are Champion-Specific

No single universal set of statistics predicts winning. Each champion has a distinct win profile shaped by their role and kit. KDA dominates for carries; mitigationRatio and totalTimeCCDealt dominate for tanks and supports. Using kills alone to evaluate performance is fundamentally flawed at the Challenger level.

Finding 2 — Six Meaningful Win-Profile Clusters Emerge

K-Means on coefficient vectors reveals six natural groups. These broadly reflect role archetypes, validating that the coefficient-based approach captures real role structure. Partial — not complete — alignment with official classifications is actually informative: complete agreement would mean we just rediscovered known labels.

Finding 3 — High Model Accuracy Across the Board

Mean AUC of 0.92 with range 0.86–0.96 across 172 champions demonstrates that in Challenger play, in-game performance stats are highly predictive of match outcome. This validates the feature set and suggests that at the highest level of play, macro-level performance (not just luck) drives results.

AUC distribution across all 172 champion models. The tight range (0.86–0.96) with mean ≈ 0.92 indicates consistent predictive power. Champions with lower AUC tend to have fewer games or more chaotic playstyles (e.g., assassins with high game-to-game variance).

// FUTURE WORK

Limitations & Next Steps

Confounding: Game State

Stats like deaths may reflect the team being behind, not be the cause of losing. Future models should incorporate game-state variables — gold differential timelines, objective control sequences — to better isolate causal vs. correlational effects.

Feature & Model Expansion

Clustering quality can be improved with richer features: itemization patterns, positional heatmaps, patch version controls. Non-linear models (gradient boosting, neural networks) could capture interaction effects invisible to logistic regression.

Practical Application: Players and coaches can use champion-specific benchmarks to set meaningful performance targets. A Jinx player should prioritize KDA and nonCsGold; a Braum player should focus on crowd control uptime and mitigationRatio — not kills. This analysis makes those distinctions data-driven and quantifiable.
